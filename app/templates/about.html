About Me

For some industries, the Internet has made existing productivity smoother and more efficient, while for others it has actually increased productivity levels (Lancioni et al, 2003). For example, the domain analysis in chapter two examines various software applications which have, themselves, arguably streamlined workflow processes which are otherwise carried out manually by staff. Many similar software systems fall under the banner of Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP), which is commonly used throughout many businesses in many different industries. ERP systems attempt to automate operations from supply-chain management, inventory control and almost any other data-oriented management process (Hitt et al, 2002).

It has however proven difficult to measure the effect an ERP has on expected returns (i.e. business profit) and whether they eventually materialise remains unknown (Hunton et al, 2003). Nevertheless, studies have offered evidence that the capital market placed incremental value on firms that adopted ERP systems, as investors reacted positively to ERP implementation announcements (Hayes et al, 2001). With regards to firm performance, Poston and Grabski (2001) examined the post implementation process of 50 ERP-adopting firms over a threeyear process. Despite mixed outcomes, their results yielded some positive outcomes such as the reduction in the ratio of employees to revenue in each of the three years.
